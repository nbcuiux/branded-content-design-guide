What is Data Mining?

Data Mining: the process of extracting new patterns and insights from data

Data mining automates detection of relevant, unsuspected relationships in a database that reports cannot effectively reveal.

In customer relationship management (CRM), data mining allows a company to concentrate their efforts on prospects that are predicted to have a larger likelihood of response.

Data Mining Techniques

• Neural networks: useful for pattern recognition or data classification, through a learning process; simulate biological systems

• Association rule: discovers interesting relationships above a threshold that reveals how strong the pattern is and how likely the rule is to recur

• Decision trees: used to extract models describing sequences of interrelated decisions or predicting future data trends

• Data visualization: visual representation of data

• Nearest neighbor method: uses class labels of nearest neighbors to determine the class label of an unknown record

• Affinity analysis: discovers co-occurrence relationships among activities performed by (or recorded about) specific individuals or groups

• Classification: maps objects or instances into distinct, predefined categories; predicts the value of an attribute based on the values of other attributes

• Segmentation: dividing a customer base into groups of individuals with specific characteristics relevant to marketing (i.e., spending habits or interests)

• Regression: predicts a number

Netflix

Leveraging big data to create tailored content that ensures success

HBO

Social Media Tracking

Canvs is working with HBO to use the power of emotions to connect with audiences

• Works with 90% of American TV networks

• Forbes estimates it made between $5-$7 million in 2016

• Clients paid between $70,000 and $500,000 for the service

Canvs can analyze reactions and posts on Facebook, Twitter, Instagram and YouTube in real-time for shows, tracking and visualizing the conversation at every second

• They’re concerned with how people are feeling when they’re watching

• Networks are using Canvs to determine how their viewers are responding

Canvs is much more detailed than quickly scanning tweets or Facebook posts because they have spent a significant amount of time building a database of "millennial" and "modern day" language (sarcasm, misspellings, conflicting emojis and hashtags, included)

• They extract emotional reactions and can currently classify 56 emotions

Canvs provdies quantitative and qualitative analyses from these feelings

• Quantitative: the higher number of emotional reactions to a show, the more passionate, loyal, and engaged the audience is

• Qualitative: break the feelings down by emotion, what that emotion is in reaction to, and what that implies for the show

Reports from Canvs have encouraged producers to either focus on or pull away from a particular character in a show or star of a reality show

The company is working on building an "impact" score, where it tracks not just emotions overall, but determines which ones drive clicks and shares.

Canvs's ability to measure and extract meaning from reactions has applications beyond television. It's proving to be useful for content creators in advertising, publishing, retail, and more.

Amazon

Pilot Season Program

Amazon is producing a number of shows under this program. They release pilot episodes and viewers give Amazon direct feedback on whether or not they would be interested in seeing more.

Based on a show's fan reaction, Amazon will choose which shows to pick up for additional episodes. This process gives Amazon a much better idea of what its audience will enjoy before it invests a full season’s worth of money into it.
